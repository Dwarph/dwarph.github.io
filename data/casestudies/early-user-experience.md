### About the project

FitXR is a VR fitness app with a library of over 1,000 classes. When I joined the growth squad, new users were downloading the app and getting lost. There was little to no onboarding — no clear place to start, no guidance through those first sessions. Just a wall of content.

That's why people were leaving. They weren't failing to convert because the product had no value — they were overwhelmed before they could feel it. And for the users who did push through, the paywall still wasn't doing enough.

The question I kept asking: what are users *actually* experiencing when they land in that library for the first time?

---

## The Challenge

You're asking someone to navigate 1,000+ classes with no map, then pay before they've felt the value. In VR, you can't rely on screenshots or reviews — people need to feel it in the headset, and they need somewhere to start.

Two fronts. Give new users a guided path through the library — hand-holding without feeling patronising. And get the paywall to speak to the person standing in front of it once they've started to find their feet.

---

## Paths of Play

### V1 — something in their hands *(March 2025)*

The first version was deliberately simple. A linear, scripted path of curated classes — the same for every user. No locking. No gating. Just: here's where to start.

Get something in front of users. Watch what happens.

Early signals were good. Users were playing more classes and completing more of them. We rolled it to 100% of English users within two weeks.

### V2 — structure *(June–August 2025)*

V1 showed us users were engaging. V2 tested whether structure could deepen that.

We added locked classes, day gating, a Box Foundations kickstart plan, content tracks by music genre and difficulty, and medals on class tiles for replayability. One golden path became a guided journey.

Before the A/B test, Zoe and I ran a round of user testing on the designs. Users understood the locked model immediately — intuitively, without explanation. But they didn't understand the unlock moment. They didn't realise they'd unlocked classes they could take outside the plan. We fixed it before we shipped.

[![Paths of Play V2 designs](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/pop-v2.png "wide")](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/pop-v2.png)

Results: average active days up. Users hitting 3+ active days jumped from 14% to 18%. FT2M held steady despite a critical bug midway through the test. The structure was working.

### V2.5 — getting out of the way *(August–October 2025)*

Here's what we built: day gating. Here's what users told us: the experience felt long and flat. Here's what the data confirmed: they were moving faster than we'd expected.

We were getting in their way.

The call was counterintuitive. Remove the gating. Trust users to move at their own pace. Stop trying to control the journey and start making it worth completing.

In place of enforced progression, we gave users earned progression: a stamp system, environments as rewards, a community mode unlock. The homescreen got a redesign. One class was cut. The whole thing got shorter and sharper.

[![Paths of Play V2.5 designs](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/pop-v2-5.png "wide")](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/pop-v2-5.png)

Users completed the plan in an average of 18 hours. We'd designed it for days.

+6ppt FT2M, statistically significant. Plan completion went from 37% to 66%. 85% of users who finished went straight into the wider library, playing an average of 8 more classes.

## The Paywall

Same question. Different screen.

The old paywall didn't know who you were. Generic copy. Generic imagery. A feature list. It was speaking to everyone, which meant it was speaking to no one. But there was a bigger problem: it didn't feel like a moment. You'd just been through an onboarding experience — your goals set, your plan revealed — and then you hit a screen that felt like a form.

Before touching the design, I spent time doing a deep dive into paywall design: how the best subscription products in the world handle this moment, what patterns work, what they have in common. The through-line was clear. The best paywalls feel *special*. They treat the decision to subscribe as something worth celebrating, not something to be rushed through.

The redesign tried to do the same. Dual column. Left side: a value list and imagery tailored to your fitness goal, with a background image branched by gender. Right side: the annual plan front and centre, pricing reframed as a weekly cost, social proof that felt real. The copy got cleaner and more direct. The whole thing got more confident.

The logic was simple. If you'd just told us you want to build strength, the paywall should say something about strength — and it should feel like the beginning of something, not a transaction.

[![Paywall redesign](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/paywall.png "wide")](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/paywall.png)

Click-through to Start Free Trial went from 55.3% to 58.8%. The strongest signal came from men on strength goals — exactly the users whose goals were now reflected back at them.

+1.3ppt uplift. ~+10% paywall conversion.

## Impact

[![Impact](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/impact.png "bleed-xl")](./data/casestudies/images/early-ux/impact.png)

Three metrics. All moved.

**+30% users playing 1+ classes.** Paths of Play gave new members a path worth following.

**+16% new user conversion.** V2.5 trusted users enough to get out of their way.

**+10% paywall conversion.** The paywall finally spoke to the person in front of it.

Three things I took away:

- **The user is the hero.** My job wasn't to build a journey and expect people to follow it. It was to understand where users were trying to go, then remove what was stopping them.
- **Cut what isn't working — even if you built it.** Day gating felt considered from our side. Users found it controlling. Building something and then cutting it, when the evidence points that way, is the job.
- **Shipping is the beginning.** V1 told us something. V2 told us something else. V2.5 came from listening to what users did with V2. If you care about your users, you don't declare victory and move on. You stay curious about what they're experiencing — and keep going back.
